Why this site?

Recently, in an interfaith gathering in Germany, one of the Christian participants asked her conversation partner from a different religious background: “So, is then my God also your God?”

In the current atmosphere of progressive globalization, everyone of us, is being exposed to different religious beliefs, the Writings of which claim to be from one God, the Supreme Being. Thus, despite all the differences between the religious systems, more and more people are asking themselves, whether these faiths come from the same one God or - from a scientifc viewpoint - from the same source. We all are more and more realizing that humanity does in fact constitute one people. So if there is only one God, and He has only one People, why are there so many different religions? Could it rather be that there is one underlying religion beneath the various religious systems? Could it be that what we call different religions are actually diverse appearances of One Common Faith? And if so, what exactly is it and how can it be made transparent?

This site is dedicated the systematic study of religion from the prespective of Oneness. If the major religions come from one source, if there is One Common Faith beneath them, it must be possible to treat and interpret their Holy Writings as if they were chapters of one and the same book, and one must come to conclusions that fit to all of them, to modern sciences and - last but not least - to general human common sense.

This site gathers data and facts from the Holy Writings as well as classical scholarly and modern scientifc literature. With transparent methodologies as prevalent in modern secular disciplines this material is condensed to demonstrate the One Common Faith underlying what we perceive to be different religions. To “stress test” this approach, the analyses are done using the Writings from Judaism, Christianity and Islam - three religious traditions presented by their clergy as distinct, superior and final revelations resulting in war and strife between them and with modern science. They are analyzed using the Writings of a new faith that appears as diametrically opposed: the Bahá’í-Faith which having no clergy, advocates the progressive revelation, relativity of religious truth and harmony of religion and science. If the existence of One Common Faith can be demonstrtated for these four, drastically different religions, it can be done for all religions.

It is hoped that the material this sites offers on the Oneness of religion, contributes to eliminating the barriers both with the the different faiths and between them and the secular world, thus facilitating their united participation “on eye level” in the global discourse. This way, religion can more effectively contribute to finding solutions to challenges humanity is facing - like hunger and poverty to name two cases. We can already produce and distribute food for the whole earth population, but large populations still suffer from hunger. This shows that these large scale challenges cannot be managed with science and technology alone. Any solution will also have to adresse the actors’ self-motivation, in this case to focus balancing collective well being and their own profit. Economic progress is, apart from science and technology, also a question of moral values which have been most effectively motivated by religion in the course of human history. So if we are to successfully solve these challenges, religion “must be part of the solution” as stated by Dr Jonathan Sacks, member of the House of Lords and former Chief Rabbi of the British Empire - or in the words of Dr Chandan Mitra, a Member of Parliament of India, “if we are aiming for a value-based society, we cannot ignore religion. Religion gives us our sins of dharma or morality.“

Methodology

This site cleraly distinguishes on the one hand between the Holy Writings (Hebrew Bible, Gospel, Koran, Bahá’í Writings) claiming to be of divine origin and, on the other hand, the established commentaries or the religious traditions that arose from them. The Holy Writings are treated here as chapters from The Same Divine Book with the passages of one Scripture being explained through recourse to the Holy Text of the other faiths. As an example verses of the Koran are interpreted with the help of the verses of the New Testament using the Book of Certitude and vice versa. These interscriptural interpretations are compiled for key tenets upheld by the major religions such as life after death, heaven, hell, resurrection, search for religious and scientific truth, etc. They are presented in a generally understandable manner so that the reader can draw his or her own conclusions and evaluate the statements made by the religious traditions and the established commentaries. This is done by compiling the relevant data and facts from the Holy Writings and well established traditional commentaries as well as modern scholarly literature. This way, those interested do not have to dig their way through the thicket of ancient commentary and modern scientific literature. The information is presented in a way that the readers, for themselves, can get an impression of what exactly the One Common Faith underlying the great religious systems is and how it harmonizes with modern secular sciences. This way the websites seeks to make transparent the One Common Faith underlying the major world religions.

The idea of religions having something in common is not new. It is already mentioned in the Holy Writings, e.g. when Christ links his Faith directly to that of Moses (Joh 5:46) or the Koran (3:64) uses the term Islam to designate both the Faith inaugurated by Muḥammad but also religion itself. The idea also has been already raised by leading scholars of the different religious backgrounds, like Rumi or most notably Clemens Alexandrinus who talks of the “One River of Truth” when referring to the common background of the Hebrew and Christian Scripture as well as the Greek philosophy. Modern scholars like Friedrich Heiler or Hans Küng also investigated and demonstrated commonalities of the major religious systems in their works. The difference of the approach here is, as set out in Bahá’u’lláhs Book of Certitude, to treat the Holy Writings as one Divine Scripture based on One Common Faith detached from man-made religious traditions. Interpretations from traditional commentaries or modern scientifc literature is only mentioned when it fits to all Holy Writings.

The Book of Certitude interprets countless passages of the Hebrew scriptures, the Gospel and the Koran to demonstrate the oneness of what we are taught to be different religions standing beside each other. According to the Book of Certitude what we see as different religions are reflections of One Single Reality or progressive revelations of One Common Divine Faith that is revealed in the course of history to an ever-evolving humanity. This happens by the appearance of “Manifestations of God” who are called prophets in most religious traditions. These Manifestations appear at different times, yet all represent the same, One God, reflect the same divine attributes, proclaim the same Faith and reveal the same Truth. Thus, it is an inadequate recognition of their station to see them as the founders of separate religions.

At its core this One Common Faith has eternal, at its periphery transitional features. The core constitutes of “the changeless faith of God, eternal in the past, eternal in the future“ dealing with virtues like compassion, love, forgiveness, reconciliation. The transitory part consists of laws and ordinances that are formulated according to the requirements of the age and adapted in the next revelation once the old laws have fulfilled their purpose and the needs of humanity have changed. Then, clinging to the old laws often results in a ritualistic repetition of ordinances and practices long after these have lost their sense. The consequence is the trivialization of religion or its being identified with superstition, prejudice and backwardness resulting in skepticism and contempt towards religious organizations and movements as we see nowadays.

Using this briefly sketched methodological approach this site will demonstrate that what we see as different religions are in fact stages in the limitless unfolding of a single Reality.

Transliteration

Words which according to Oxford English Dictionary have become part of the English language such as Rabbi, Imam, Hajj, Hosanna, Koran, Haftorah, Medina, Shiraz, Sheikh, Menora etc. are not transliterated. Transliteration of Hebrew names and terms follows Jenni, Westermann: Theologisches Handwörterbuch zum Alten Testament, Greek Names from the New Testament are translietrated according to Balz, Schneider: Exegetisches Wörterbuch zum Neuen Testament.

Arabic and Persian words are transliterated according to the Bahá’í Transliteration System which goes back to a system propsed on the 10th International Congress of Orientalists held in Geneva in 1894 and was adopted by the Royal Asiatic Society in July 1894. The system was altered by Shoghi Effendi, introduced in the Bahá’í World in the mid twenties of the lst century. Later it was detailed out on behalf of the Universal House of Justice. These changes are meant to make the the system more intuitive and easy to use for a wide audience - having Western or Oriental background - with no systematic or academic training in Oriental Languages while making sure that specialists also easily recognize the Arabic or Persian terms. Due to these alterations it has some features distnguishing it from the conventional transliteration systems used by specialists in Araic or Perisan:

  • It supports transliteration of both Arabic and Persian while indicating in minor ways the difference between Arabic and Persian usages. Thus, the same term might be transliterated differently depending whether the context of its usage is Arabic or Persian (see below Nr. 8)
  • When the perfect transliteration is misleading to laymen as to the the correct pronunciation, preference is given to the authentic pronunciation, e.g. in case of the assimilation of the definite Arabic article (see below Nr. 5)
  • Where the original system permitted alternative transliterations, the Shoghi Effendi choose the character which are easiest to reproduced on typewriters and - as a result - modern WYSIWYG editors, e.g. kitáb, instead of kitāb.

  1. The ْ (sukún) is not transliterated
  2. In case of consonant doubling the ّ (shadda, Pers. tashdíd) is transliterated by doubling the respective consonant, e.g. awwal, Pers. avval for أول
  3. A strong hamza (hamzatu’l-qaṭ‘) in the medial or final position is transliterated with a ’.Exception is Bahá both standalone as well as part of proper names, i.e. Bahá instead of Bahá’ for بهاء, ‘Abdu’l-Bahá instead of ‘Abdu’l-Bahá’ for عبد البهاء or Jináb-i-Bahá instead of Jináb-i-Bahá’ für جناب بهاء. The reason is that the final hamza is omitted in Persian when followed by a long á sound and this has become common practice in Bahá'í literaure for the word Bahá. The inital hamzatu’l-qaṭ‘ is not transliterated, as in written Arabic and Persian, it is usually invisible, along with its short vowel, e.g. i‘jáz instead of ’i‘jáz for إعجاز.
  4. Words that start with initial elidable hamza, hamzatu’l-waṣl, do not have the transliterated hamza symbol ’, e.g. ism instead of ’ism' for ﺍﺳﻡ. When the elidable hamza drops out, its alif seat remains in spelling, but it gets a different symbol on top of it, called a waṣla, which indicates deletion of the glottal stop and liaison between the previous vowel and the following consonant. If a word starting with elidable hamza is preceded by a consonant, a auxiliary vowel is affixed to the consonant in order to facilitate pronunciation. The alif with the waṣla is transliterated as ’, the auxiliary vowel is transliterated as part of the preceeding word, e.g. umm (mother) and kitáb (book) give in the nominative form ummu'l-kitáb for mother book for ام الکتاب. Or the first verse of Bahá'u'lláhs Fire Tablet is transliterated as mit qad iḥtaraqa’l-mukhliṣún for قد احترق المخلصون.
  5. The l of the definite article al- is assimilated to certain consonants (ت, ث, د, ذ, ر, ز, س, ش, ص, ض, ط, ظ, ل, ن) in the beginning of words. This is transliterated by doubling the respective consonant, e.g. Imam aṣ-Ṣádiq or ash-Shams for sun.
  6. The transliteration is done fully vocalized except for prper names and the end of sentences or phrases, where the pause form is used, i.e. bi-smi’lláhi’r-raḥmáni’r-raḥím für بسم الله رحمن الرحیم instead of bi-smi’lláhi’r-raḥmáni’r-raḥími, or Muḥammad for محمد. This also applies to titles given to leading religious personalities by their coummunities, e.g. Siyyidu’sh-Shuhadá’, for the third shiite Imam Ḥusayn or Ummu’l-Mu’minín, the Titel of ‘Á’isha.
  7. The suffixed possessive pronoun (hu,hi) is transliterated as long vowels (hú,hí) after short syllables, e.g. kitábuhú for کتابه and shí‘atihí for شیعته
  8. Technical terms are transliterated in the Arabic form, e.g. an-núr al-muḥammadí for النور المحمدیinstead of the persianized núr-i-muḥammadí. Exception are terms that have been developed in a mainly Persian environment such as dalíl-i-tqarír, because Persians, when using Arabic words do not observe Arabic grammatical and phonetic rules. This is also done for Names of Persians after 1500, as in this is the beginning of the Safavid Dynasty and the end of Iran's being ruled by Arab caliphs and their vasalls (EI2 Irán), e.g. Salmán al-Farisí (First Persian Muslim) instead of Salmán-i-Farsí, and Naṣir-i’d-Dín Sháh instead of Naṣir-u’d-Dín Sháh as required by Arabic grammatical form.
  9. The silent wáw in Persian words is transliterated, e.g. Khwárazm for خوارزم.
  10. Whenever the individual parts of Persian Names are connected by an iḍáfih, this is transliterated by a -, e.g. Kitáb-i-Íqán, the Persian title of the Book of Certitude.
